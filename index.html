Why This Exists

The Mission Behind the Game

Here's an uncomfortable truth: the same psychological wiring that pulls people toward gambling — the need to be right, the thrill of prediction, the dopamine hit of a correct call — exists in every one of us. The platforms built to capture that impulse are optimized for one thing: keeping you spending.

Nobody asked what happens if you redirect that energy instead of fight it. Odds God is the answer. Same competitive drive. Same satisfaction of a confirmed call. No money in, no money out — and your accuracy funds real change in the world.

The numbers are not abstract. An estimated 10 million Americans meet criteria for problem gambling. The fastest-growing segment is 18–34 year olds, normalized by sports betting apps built to look like games. Odds God doesn't lecture anyone. It offers a better game.

The prediction data matters too. Crowd-sourced accuracy has genuine research value — more accurate than polls, more honest than punditry. Odds God captures that intelligence and makes it available to researchers and institutions who want to understand what people actually believe will happen.

Resolution

How Answers Are Determined

Every Season 1 question has a verifiable, publicly documented correct answer. No judgment calls. No editorial discretion.

📰

Primary Source Confirmation

Questions resolve based on confirmed reporting from major news organizations, official government statements, SEC filings, or primary public documentation. Not rumor. Not speculation. Confirmed fact only.

⚡

Rolling Resolution

Questions resolve the moment the real-world event is confirmed — not all at once at season end. When GPT-5 launched March 24, that question resolved March 24. You'll be notified by email if you provided one.

🔒

Hard Deadline — June 30, 2026

Any question still open on June 30, 2026 resolves at 11:59 PM Pacific based on best available evidence. No extensions. The season is the season.

📋

Public Resolution Log

Every resolved question is logged publicly with source documentation. Every ruling is verifiable. Transparency isn't optional here — it's the whole point.
